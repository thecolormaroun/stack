# Maroun's Design Philosophy

Distilled design beliefs extracted from Maroun's Zettelkasten. THE definitive reference for how Maroun thinks about design.

---

## Visual Design Philosophy

### Magic Over Efficiency
> "When everything is measured, beauty starts to feel like waste. Many teams treat visual expression as an indulgence, not an advantage. That's a mistake. People don't fall in love with logic, they fall in love with presence, feeling, or 'vibes'."

Maroun believes the industry optimized so hard for efficiency that we killed the magic. Brands like Linear, Framer, Notion invest in visual identity as competitive differentiation. **Aesthetics signal maturity.**

### Taste as Competitive Advantage
> "Taste is developing a refined sense of judgment and finding the balance that produces a pleasing and integrated whole." — Ken Kocienda

Taste isn't personal preference—it's cultivated judgment. It develops through exposure and practice. Taste asks: *"Does this create a pleasing whole?"* not just *"Does this work?"*

The gap between taste and skill is painful but necessary. You know good work before you can make it. Volume closes the gap.

### Visual Design Gets You Hired
Visual design is currently overvalued due to scarcity—too many "UX designers" with weak visual skills. Market reality: hiring managers screen visual first. Lead portfolio with strongest visual work. UX thinking matters more long-term, but visual opens doors.

## UX/Interaction Philosophy

### Design Is How It Works
> "Design is not just what it looks like and feels like. Design is how it works." — Steve Jobs via Kocienda

Visual appearance is merely the surface expression of deeper design decisions. Designers must be involved from the earliest stages—not decorating what engineers build, but determining what gets created.

### One Thing at a Time
Present users with a single clear action or piece of information at each step. Cognitive overload kills momentum. When users face multiple competing actions, they freeze or make poor decisions.

### Design for System 1
> "Most tools for thought assume users operate in System 2 mode—rational, deliberate. In reality, most daily work happens in System 1—reactive, panicky, just trying to keep up."

Your users aren't thinking deeply. They're over-promised, late to meetings, struggling with their inbox. Design for overwhelmed, reactive users—not idealized ones.

### Calm Confidence Over Delight
> "We don't design for delight—we design to create calm and confidence. Respect the user and the seriousness of their task."

Users come to serious products feeling anxious. Playful delight feels dismissive of their stress. Design for calm that says: *"This matters, and we'll help you get it right."*

### Address Emotional Barriers
There's a bigger emotional challenge than a functional challenge. You can have perfect UX functionally and users still abandon if emotional barriers are too high—anxiety, shame, overwhelm are UX problems.

### Progress and Celebration
- **Build momentum**: Show users their progress with every action. Visible progress creates psychological momentum.
- **Create celebratory moments**: Break work into thematic sections. When complete, create pauses that let users feel great about what they've done.

### Guidance, Not Expertise
> "The product should be a sherpa, guide, or coach—not an expert, advisor, or parent. You're the protagonist of your own story."

Users resent being talked down to. The right tone empowers users while providing expert guidance.

## Typography Beliefs

### Typography Exists to Honor Content
> "Typography exists to honor content." — Robert Bringhurst

All typographic decisions serve one purpose: reveal and honor the text's meaning, not showcase the designer's skill. Typography should be invisible—good typography = effortless reading.

### Core Typography Rules
- **Read the text before designing it** — can't honor what you don't understand
- **Discover outer logic in inner logic** — the text tells you how it wants to be designed
- **66 characters per line** is ideal measure
- **Single word space between sentences** — not two
- **Indent only if preceded by another paragraph**
- **Block quotes**: Change ONE thing (face, size, or indent)—not all three

### Use REM, Not PX
Pixels are fixed and ignore user preferences. REM respects accessibility settings and user agency. This single choice dramatically improves accessibility.

## Systems Thinking

### Systems Enable Excellence at Scale
> "We make long-term investments into brand, UI patterns, and process such that we never lose track of quality, craft, and delight at scale. With great assets, we can focus on composition." — Henry Modisett

Systems free designers to focus on composition rather than construction. Well-designed systems let you have both speed and craft.

### Build Once, Use Everywhere
Build components with explicit goal of reusing them everywhere. Building for one use case is fast now, slow later. Building for reuse is slower now, exponentially faster later. Tipping point: usually after 3-4 uses.

### Atomic Design
Break interfaces into fundamental building blocks: Atoms → Molecules → Organisms → Templates → Pages. Understanding this hierarchy enables systematic, scalable design.

### UX and UI Cannot Be Separated
> "Your UI directly affects the UX. Visual design isn't decorative—it's functional."

The idea of separating UX and UI into different roles is outdated. Button hierarchy affects what users click. Color draws attention. Typography affects trust. Integrated ownership enables faster iteration.

## AI + Design Intersection

### Understand Your Materials
> "I need to understand the material I'm working with in the same way that carpenters understand wood." — Maggie Appleton

Designers working with AI must develop technical literacy. You can only design interactions you can conceptualize. Novel interface patterns emerge when designers understand the underlying system deeply.

### Vertical Over Horizontal
> "If you tell a user they can do anything, they will do nothing."

Open-ended chat boxes are a temporary blip. Vertical interfaces designed for specific professional workflows are where AI truly excels. Design AI tools around specific workflows, not general-purpose chat.

### Beyond the Prompt Box
Open-ended prompt boxes place all cognitive burden on users. Better AI interfaces guide users through specific workflows toward specific goals. Dynamic, adaptive interfaces that morph based on context are the future.

### Design for the Abstraction Layer
The more you abstract AI capabilities, the less control users have. The less you abstract, the more complex it becomes. Find the right level through iteration—help users establish mental models for AI behavior, not understand technical details.

## Design Leadership Philosophy

### Head of Design = CEO of Design Org
Three types of leadership required:
1. **Creative** — vision, quality bar, experience principles
2. **Managerial** — team tone, growth opportunities, feedback
3. **Operational** — communications, tools, scheduling

Common mistake: favoring creative leadership over managerial/operational. Creative vision without operational excellence = flailing team.

### Craft-Based Management
> "Design management has perhaps devolved into paper pushing. Let's get good at product. Let's get good at talking about design." — Mig Reyes

Managers earn trust through craft knowledge, not administrative processes. Consider yourself the studio head of your team—accountable for your team's entire body of work.

### Hands-On Leadership
> "A leader should be a chef. Still in the kitchen. Not chopping carrots, but if somebody cuts their fingers, she can take over the sauce station." — Alex Schleifer

Design leaders who remain active practitioners maintain credibility, quality standards, and team connection. This enables smaller, flatter teams.

### The Power of No
> "Design work should only be done when adequately prioritized and staffed, and when there is time to develop quality solutions."

A design organization is only as good as what it delivers. Producing crap = design associated with crap. Protect quality by saying no to under-resourced work.

### Think Horizontally
> "We can't expect designers to be thinking horizontally if they don't know what's happening next door."

Multiple teams can simultaneously stumble on the same customer problem. Design must serve as the unifying voice that thinks across the organization.

## What Maroun Values

- **Taste** — cultivated judgment over personal preference
- **Craft** — excellence in execution, attention to detail
- **Magic** — beauty and feeling, not just function
- **Systems** — leverage through reusability and consistency
- **Calm confidence** — respecting user's emotional state
- **Technical literacy** — understanding your materials
- **Hands-on leadership** — leading through craft, not just management

## What Maroun Rejects

- **Efficiency over magic** — optimizing away beauty
- **Process over craft** — methodology as proxy for quality
- **Decoration** — visual design as afterthought
- **UX/UI split** — treating UI as skinning
- **Delight in serious contexts** — being cute when users are anxious
- **Open-ended interfaces** — dumping cognitive load on users
- **Paper-pushing management** — administration over craft leadership
